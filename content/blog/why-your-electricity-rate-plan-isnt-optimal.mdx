Most homes in Georgia are on the default flat-rate plan. For most, that's fine. For about a third of households, it's leaving real money on the table — sometimes hundreds of dollars a year.

The problem is that picking a rate plan well requires knowing your hourly usage shape, the fixed fees of every alternative, and the seasonal rate structures. Nobody has time for that.

## What we look at

When Utility Pilot evaluates a plan switch, it asks three questions:

1. What does your usage actually look like, hour by hour?
2. What does each available plan charge across that exact pattern?
3. What's the fixed monthly fee — and does it eat the savings?

The third one trips most people up. A "cheaper" rate per kWh can lose to a higher rate plan with no monthly customer charge, depending on how much you use.

## The honest answer

For a lot of households, the savings from switching are small — $5 to $15 a month. That's still real money over a decade, but it's not life-changing.

The ones who benefit most are heavy summer users (think: pool pumps, electric heat, large homes) who can shift load to overnight hours. Time-of-use plans reward that pattern hard.

If you're not sure which bucket you're in, run your last 12 months through the analyzer and see what falls out.
