The economics of residential solar in Georgia just got more complicated. Here's the short version.

## What changed

Net metering — the policy where your meter spins backward when you export to the grid — has been narrowed. New residential solar installations after the program cap was reached are no longer compensated at the full retail rate for excess generation. Instead, exports are credited at a wholesale-like avoided-cost rate, which is roughly a third of retail.

For homes that consume most of what they generate during the day, this barely matters. For homes that overproduce — large arrays, low daytime usage, no battery — the export credit drop can extend payback by 4–7 years.

## What still works

Three configurations still pencil out reasonably well:

- **Right-sized arrays.** Sized to roughly match daytime self-consumption, not annual consumption.
- **Solar + battery.** Storing midday excess to use in the evening avoids the export-rate haircut entirely.
- **Heat-pump conversions paired with solar.** Shifting heating load from gas to electric increases daytime use and improves self-consumption ratios.

## What we'd avoid

Oversized grid-tied systems with no battery, sold on the assumption that you'll be made whole on exports. Under the new rules, you won't be — and the model the salesperson is showing you may still assume the old policy.

If you're getting quotes, ask explicitly which net-metering tariff your home will be enrolled under, and run the numbers using that compensation rate.
